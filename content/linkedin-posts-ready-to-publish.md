A Tier-1 auto component plant called us three years ago.

Their zinc phosphate line was running perfectly by every metric they measured — free acid in spec, total acid in spec, temperature correct.

But their coating weight was dropping batch by batch. Salt spray failures were increasing.

Nobody could figure it out. Their chemical supplier's rep suggested increasing concentration. It didn't help.

We visited the plant and spent two hours watching the line run.

The problem had nothing to do with the chemistry.

Their rinsing sequence had been shortened to increase throughput. The activation stage was being bypassed on every third batch to save time.

The bath chemistry was perfect. The process around it was broken.

This is the single most important thing I have learned in four decades of process chemistry:

The chemical is almost never the problem. The process always is.

If you are dealing with inconsistent salt spray results and your titration logs look fine — don't change the chemical. Look at the process around it.

Happy to discuss specific situations in the comments.

## WEEK 2 POSTS

### Company Page — Monday: "Process Insight"
**Format:** 200-word text + attach an infographic (create in Canva)

The 4 parameters that actually control zinc phosphate crystal quality — and which 2 your titration sheet is missing.

Most phosphating lines track free acid and total acid. Both matter. But they're the slow-moving parameters — they drift over hours, and twice-daily titration is adequate to catch them.

The two parameters that move fast — and that can ruin a batch before you notice — are:

**Temperature.** Can drift 3–5°C in 20 minutes if a heating element starts to fail. A 4°C drop reduces reaction kinetics enough to change crystal morphology. Your titration numbers will look fine.

**Accelerator concentration.** Consumed with every batch. On a high-throughput line, concentration can drop 20% between manual additions. The coating forms, but it's porous. Salt spray performance is 30–40% lower than expected.

The fix isn't complex. A thermocouple with a data logger costs under ₹8,000. It tells you what the bath temperature was for every batch you've ever run.

If you're investigating inconsistent salt spray results, start here before changing the chemical.

### Company Page — Thursday: "Industry Signal"

India's Tier-1 automotive exports to the EU are growing — and so is the regulatory pressure on chromium.

The EU ELV Directive exemption for hexavalent chromium (Cr-VI) in surface treatment is under review. When it tightens, every Indian auto component exporter with European customers will need to demonstrate Cr-VI-free passivation — not just intention, but validated process data.

Most Indian suppliers are aware of this. Fewer have a concrete transition plan.

The challenge isn't just swapping the chemical. Cr-free passivation systems behave differently — different bath chemistry, different performance envelope, different PPAP requirements. The process changes, not just the product.

If you're a Tier-1 or Tier-2 exporter and this isn't on your near-term roadmap, it should be.
